There's something powerful about letting people watch you work. Not the polished, curated version — the real thing, with all its false starts and dead ends.

## Why build in public?

Building in public isn't about performance. It's about accountability and connection. When you share your process, you invite others into your thinking. You create surface area for serendipity.

I've found that the act of explaining what I'm doing forces me to understand it better. Writing about a problem often reveals the solution. And the feedback loop from sharing — even to a small audience — keeps me moving forward on days when motivation is low.

## The uncomfortable parts

Let's be honest: it's not always comfortable. There's a vulnerability in showing work that isn't finished. You worry about being judged for the messy middle, for the ideas that don't pan out.

But I've learned that most people aren't judging. They're relating. They're seeing their own struggles reflected back. And that's where the real value lives — not in the finished product, but in the shared experience of making something.

## What I'm trying

Right now, I'm experimenting with writing more frequently about smaller things. Not every post needs to be a manifesto. Sometimes a few paragraphs about something I noticed or learned is enough.

The goal isn't to build an audience. It's to build a practice.
