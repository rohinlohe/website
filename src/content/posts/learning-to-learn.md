The most useful skill I've developed isn't any specific technology or technique. It's learning how to learn.

## The myth of talent

We love stories about naturals — people who just "get it" from the start. But the more I talk to people who are excellent at what they do, the more I realize they all have one thing in common: they've gotten very good at the process of learning itself.

They know when to read and when to practice. They know how to break down a complex skill into smaller pieces. They know when they're stuck and how to get unstuck.

## What works for me

**Start with the whole, then zoom in.** I like to get a rough sense of the entire landscape before diving into details. This gives me a mental map that helps me place new information as I encounter it.

**Teach what you learn.** Explaining something to someone else is the fastest way to find the gaps in your understanding. If I can't explain it simply, I don't understand it well enough.

**Embrace confusion.** The uncomfortable feeling of not understanding is a signal that learning is happening. I've trained myself to lean into that feeling instead of running from it.

## The long game

Learning compounds. The more you know, the easier it is to learn the next thing, because you have more mental models to connect new information to. The early stages are the hardest. But if you stick with the process, it gets easier — and more rewarding — over time.
