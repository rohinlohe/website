I've been thinking a lot about craft lately — what it means to care about how something is made, not just what it does.

## The difference between good and great

Good work solves the problem. Great work solves the problem in a way that feels inevitable, like it couldn't have been done any other way. That feeling of inevitability is the hallmark of craft.

It shows up in software when the API feels natural. In writing when a sentence lands perfectly. In cooking when the flavors are balanced without any single one dominating. You can't always articulate what makes it work, but you can feel it.

## Curiosity as fuel

I think craft is sustained by curiosity. The craftsperson asks "what if?" and "why?" long after everyone else has moved on. They notice the details that others skip. They find the extra 10% that transforms something from functional to delightful.

Curiosity keeps you from settling. It makes you tear apart something that's working fine because you sense it could be better. That's uncomfortable, but it's where the growth happens.

## The practice

Craft isn't a destination. It's a daily practice of paying attention — to your tools, to your materials, to the people who will use what you make. Some days the work flows. Other days it's a grind. But showing up either way is the whole game.
